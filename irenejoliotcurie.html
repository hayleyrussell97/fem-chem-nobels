Irene Joliot-Curie

Nobel Prize Winner 1935

Irene was born in paris in 1897 to Henry and Marie Curie.

Unsatisfied with the quality of the loal education, her mother and Paul Langevin formed a cooperative where 9 scholars taught each other's children.

She went on to work as a nurse during the first world war and completed her education at the Radium Institute, founded by her parents.

At the end her PhD she was asked to teach some lab skills to Frederic Joliot, whom she later wed taking on the name Irene Joliot-Curie. Presumably, this was less of a problem with HR back in the day.

Irene and her husband were awarded the 1935 Nobel prize for their discovery of beta emission, the process by which an unstable radioactive isotope is formed by a stable isotope losing a positron.

They also participated in the creation of the first French nuclear reactor in 1948.

She died in 1956 from leukemia, believed to have been caused by radioactive exposure.
